Temporal trends in marine biodiversity - a global synthesis

The extent to which the well-known global declines in biodiversity are reflected at local scales is unclear.  In a synthesis of 439 time-series, we show that local species richness declined in areas of intense human impact, but increased in many areas without documented local anthropogenic drivers of biodiversity change.  Read more...

Climate warming, body size decline, and demographic models

Recent empirical work in terrestrial and freshwater communities suggests that body size decline may be a general response to climate warming.  In this research I test this prediction using a temperate stony coral, and use model simulations to explore the influence of temperature on body size and population growth rate.  Read more...

Resource use at the local scale – the role of diversity, top-down control, and indirect effects

Space is the limiting resource for sessile organisms in nearshore rocky habitats, and in this research I explored how sessile biodiversity and grazers interact to influence the structure and function of subtidal rock walls in the Salish Sea (the inland waters of the Pacific Northwest).  Read more...

Water flow and mesoscale patterns of subtidal diversity

It is important to evaluate the results of experimental fieldwork conducted at necessarily small spatial scales within a broader spatial context.  In this research, I questioned the role of ‘mesoscale’ (10-100 km) oceanographic variation on the diversity of subtidal rock walls in the Salish Sea.  Read more...

Four decades, few changes: are subtidal rock walls refuges of biodiversity?

Many marine ecosystems, including coral reefs and kelp forests, have undergone dramatic transitions from structurally complex and biologically rich communities to structurally simple and depauperate habitats often dominated by weedy algae.  In this study we asked: how and why have subtidal rock wall communities in the San Juan Islands changed over four decades?   Read more...

Physiological consequences of age in corals

Many tropical reef corals are colonial and are capable of undergoing fission, fusion, and fragmentation.  This means that age and size are not necessarily related - and I explored the physiological consequences of this disconnect during my masters research.  Read more...

Physiological ecology of mushroom corals (Fungia spp.)

Mushroom corals (Fungia spp.) are an enigmatic group of tropical reef corals.  Unlike most corals, Fungia species are not colonial, but solitary organisms.  Therefore, age and size are correlated and individual size is a useful predictor of physiological and ecological traits.  I examined physiological constraints on maximum size, and the physiological costs of group living.  Read more...
