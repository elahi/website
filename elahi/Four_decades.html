Four decades, few changes: are subtidal rock walls refuges of biodiversity?

It is well known that humans are modifying the environment in many ways, including climate change, non-native species introductions, and habitat protection.  Many marine ecosystems, including coral reefs and kelp forests, have undergone dramatic transitions from structurally complex and biologically rich communities to structurally simple and depauperate habitats often dominated by weedy algae.  Field studies that span several decades are rare, but important when trying to evaluate the role of human activities on the environment.  In this study we asked: how and why have subtidal rock wall communities in the San Juan Islands changed over four decades?  


Comparison of a historic and a modern quadrat.

An archive of underwater photographs taken by former University of Washington graduate student Charles Birkeland between 1969 and 1974 was compared with my own photographs at the same study site between 2008 and 2011.  Surprisingly, the historical communities exhibited lower biodiversity (fewer species) than the modern communities.  This result is likely related to intense grazing by animals like chitons (snail relatives), rather than species introductions in modern times.  Modern and historic communities exhibited similar stability, and thus the data suggest that there has been limited change over four decades, despite a clear increase in temperature (due to climate change) and fish abundance (due to the establishment of marine protected areas).  The lack of change in these rock wall communities may be related to their vertical topography, which limits physical disturbance and prevents weedy algae from taking over space.
